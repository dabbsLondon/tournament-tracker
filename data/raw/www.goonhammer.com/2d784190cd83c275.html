          Thoughts<\/h3>\n

          This game was streamed – You can watch the coverage of it on Games Workshop’s Twitch stream.\u00a0Click here for that link.<\/a><\/strong><\/p>\n

          Rob: <\/strong>The T’au have the edge here in most respects – they outspeed, out-range, and out-shoot the Leagues of Votann, and they just have more units to work with. Junior has to play cautiously with the Hekaton Land Fortresses or risk losing them early. The T’au player is more than capable of zipping across the table to light them up with Markerlight observers at any point; it’s just a question of getting line of sight on them with the Riptides and tanks, avoiding being shot back, and keeping the Votann deep striking in front of their army and not behind. Glyn’s T’au army is vehicle-heavy and that makes the Graviton Cannons pretty nasty into targets like the Riptides.<\/p>\n

          Glyn ended up going second and was able to control the board early; Yaegirs are easy pickings for the T’au indirect fire, leading to Glyn being able to remove nearly all of them – five units – before Junior’s second turn, leaving him with only two Hekatons, a mortar unit, and a single battle-shocked Yaegir on the table going into his second turn. For Junior the play is to then try and take out more valuable units but the Leagues of Votann generally struggle with unit counts late in the game.<\/p>\n

          Of course this all ignores the elephant in the room, which is that the Mont’ka Detachment has the single tool that completely shuts down the Hearthguard: Counterfire Defence Systems can give any T’au Empire unit -1 damage on incoming attacks, and that makes those units of Hearthguard completely worthless when it comes to their shooting as it halves their output. Because they’ll be in Deep Strike it’s easy to plan and prepare for their arrival, and that’s exactly what Glyn did. Once the transports are gone, the T’au are more or less free to pick apart the Votann army and this game was over pretty early.<\/p>\n
